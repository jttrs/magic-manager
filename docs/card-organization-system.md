# Magic: The Gathering Card Organization System

## Goals

1. **Protect high-value cards** for long-term investment and preservation.
2. **Keep all cards accessible** for Commander and constructed deck building.
3. **Encourage creative discovery** — stumbling onto synergies and forgotten gems
   while browsing should be a natural byproduct of the system.
4. **Scale gracefully** from hundreds of cards to tens of thousands without
   requiring a full reorganization.

---

## Philosophy

A player's collection serves a fundamentally different purpose than a store's
inventory. A store needs to locate a specific card by name in seconds. A player
needs to *browse by function* — "what three-drop blue creatures do I own?" — and
wants the browsing itself to spark ideas.

Over-organizing (alphabetical within set within color) optimizes for retrieval
speed at the expense of creative serendipity. Under-organizing (one big box)
makes everything inaccessible. The system described here sits deliberately in
the middle: structured enough to find what you need, loose enough to surprise
you with what you forgot you had.

The key insight is that **deck building follows a predictable mental workflow**:

1. Pick a theme or commander.
2. Pull high-impact rares and mythics by color.
3. Fill the mana curve with commons and uncommons by color and card type.
4. Tune the ratio of creatures to non-creatures.

The organization mirrors this workflow exactly.

## The CCNC System

CCNC stands for **Color → Creature/Non-Creature → Casting Cost**. Every card in
the collection is sorted through these layers in order. The depth of sorting
depends on the card's tier.

### Sorting Layers

| Layer | Description | Example Buckets |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| **C** — Color | The card's color identity | White, Blue, Black, Red, Green, Multicolor, Colorless, Lands |
| **C** — Creature / Non-Creature | Whether the card is a creature spell or not | Creatures, Non-Creatures |
| **N** — *(reserved for future use)* | — | — |
| **C** — Casting Cost | The card's mana value | 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7+ |

Within any single bucket (e.g. "Blue Creatures, MV 3") cards are **not**
alphabetized or sorted by set. This is intentional — flipping through a loose
stack surfaces forgotten cards and cross-set synergies that strict alphabetical
order would hide.

## Card Tiers and Storage

Cards are separated into three physical tiers based on value and role. Each tier
uses different storage and a different depth of CCNC sorting.

### Tier 1 — High-Value Investment Cards

**What goes here:** Cards worth roughly $10+ (adjust threshold to your
comfort), Reserved List staples, foils or special printings you are holding
long-term.

**Storage:**

- Inner sleeve (perfect-fit) + top-loader, or inner sleeve + side-loading
  binder pages to prevent cards sliding out.
- House in a zippered portfolio or high-quality ring binder (Ultra Pro Zippered
  PRO-Binder, Vault X, or similar).
- Store in a climate-controlled area away from direct sunlight and humidity.

**Sorting depth:** Color → Creature / Non-Creature. No further breakdown needed
since the volume per binder is small enough to browse quickly.

**Tracking:** Maintain a digital inventory (spreadsheet, Moxfield, Archidekt, or
Delver Lens) with card name, set, condition, and approximate value. This
doubles as insurance documentation.

**Playing with investment cards:** If a Tier 1 card is also in an active deck,
use a high-quality proxy in the deck and keep the real card protected in the
binder. Most playgroups accept proxies for cards you own. Alternatively,
double-sleeve (perfect-fit inner + outer) any Tier 1 card that goes into a
played deck.

### Tier 2 — Playable Rares, Mythics, and Powerful Uncommons

**What goes here:** Rares and mythics below the investment threshold, plus
uncommons powerful enough to headline a strategy (e.g. Swords to Plowshares,
Counterspell, Lightning Bolt).

**Storage:**

- Standard side-loading binder pages in a ring binder or portfolio.
- One binder per color, plus binders for Multicolor, Colorless, and Lands.
  Consolidate into fewer binders if volume doesn't justify one per color.

**Sorting depth:** Color → Creature / Non-Creature. Cards are slipped into pages
without further ordering. When building a deck, pull the binder for the relevant
color and flip through — the semi-random order is a feature, not a bug.

### Tier 3 — Bulk Commons and Uncommons

**What goes here:** Everything else.

**Storage:**

- BCW or Ultra Pro long boxes (800-count or 1000-count).
- Labeled cardboard or plastic dividers between each bucket.

**Sorting depth:** Full CCNC — Color → Creature / Non-Creature → Casting Cost.
Label dividers clearly (e.g. "Blue / Creatures / 3 MV"). This is where the
deepest sorting pays off, because the volume is highest and mana-curve browsing
is the primary use case.

## Deck Idea Staging

Keep a small box or a reserved section for **deck idea piles**. When an idea
strikes during sorting or browsing — a commander, a theme, a two-card combo —
pull the relevant cards and drop them into a labeled pile. This captures
inspiration without interrupting the organizing flow. Ideas that don't
materialize get filed back during the next sorting session.

---

## Practical Sorting Tips

- **Sort color first across the entire batch** before doing any finer
  breakdown. This is the highest-leverage pass.
- **Flip cards upside-down** when splitting creatures from non-creatures. Power
  and toughness become visible at the top edge, making the split nearly
  instantaneous.
- **Work in sessions, not marathons.** An hour of focused sorting per day is
  sustainable. Trying to power through thousands of cards in one sitting leads
  to misfiles and burnout.
- **Process new acquisitions immediately.** When cards come in (packs, trades,
  orders), sort them into the system the same day. A small "inbox" box is fine
  as a buffer, but don't let it grow into a second unsorted collection.
- **Re-evaluate tier thresholds periodically.** A card that was bulk last year
  may be a $15 staple today. When you notice price movement, promote or demote
  cards between tiers.

## Pitfalls to Avoid

These are common organizational choices that serve different goals (store
inventory, speculation, competitive tournament play) and actively work against
the player-brewer goals of this system.

### Alphabetizing within color

Optimizes for "find this exact card by name" retrieval, which is a store
concern. For a player, it destroys browse-and-discover value and adds
significant maintenance cost every time new cards enter the collection.

### Sorting by set or expansion

Useful for collectors tracking set completion or stores managing inventory by
product line. For deck building it is nearly useless — you don't think "I need a
Theros creature" when building a deck, you think "I need a white three-drop
creature."

### Sorting by rarity alone

Rarity is already handled by the tier system. Within a tier, rarity provides no
deck-building signal. A powerful uncommon and a bulk rare in the same color and
cost bracket serve the same role in a deck.

### Keeping cards in set-based storage (booster boxes, bundles, fat packs)

This is nostalgia masquerading as organization. Cards trapped in sealed-product
packaging are invisible to the deck-building process. If the cards are worth
protecting, tier them properly. If the sealed product itself is the investment,
that is a separate collecting concern — keep it sealed and stored elsewhere.

### Using a single "trade binder" as catch-all organization

A trade binder organized for showing off to trade partners is not organized for
building decks. Maintain a separate trade binder if you trade actively, but
don't let it become the de facto home for your best playable cards.

### Sleeving everything in the collection

Penny-sleeving ten thousand bulk commons is expensive, time-consuming, and makes
browsing slower. Reserve sleeves for Tier 1 (investment) and active decks. Tier
2 binder pages provide adequate protection. Tier 3 cards in boxes are fine
unsleeved.

### Over-investing in storage before sorting

Buying a dozen premium binders before knowing your volume per color leads to
half-empty binders and wasted money. Sort first into temporary boxes, measure
volume, then buy storage that fits.

## Summary

| Tier | Contents | Storage | CCNC Depth | Sleeving |
|------|----------|---------|------------|----------|
| 1 | High-value ($10+) | Zippered binder, top-loaders | Color → Type | Inner + top-loader or double-sleeve |
| 2 | Playable rares/mythics/strong uncommons | Side-load binder pages | Color → Type | Binder page is sufficient |
| 3 | Bulk commons/uncommons | Long boxes with dividers | Color → Type → MV | None |

The system is designed to be **built once and maintained incrementally**. New
cards slot into the correct tier and bucket on arrival. Periodic reviews
promote or demote cards between tiers as values shift. The structure mirrors
how you actually build decks, so the act of browsing your collection *is* the
first step of deck building.
